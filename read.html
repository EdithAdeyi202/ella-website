Yam plants are herbaceous annual or perennials with climbing or trailing vines. The vines can be smooth or prickly, reaching 10 m (32.8 ft) or more in length depending on the variety. The leaves of the plant are simple and usually oval to heart-shaped with petioles which are the same length, or slightly longer, than the leaf blade itself. Some varieties possess spikes at the bases of the leaves. The tubers can be cylindrical, curved or lobed, with brown, grey, black or pink skin and white, orange or purplish flesh. Most yams are annual plants, harvested after one season, but some are perennial with tubers increasing in size each year with the vines dying back at the end of the growing season and regrowing on the return of favorable conditions. The origin of yams is uncertain and genetic information suggests that there may be more than one point of origin.
